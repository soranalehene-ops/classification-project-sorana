Which EDA visualization surfaced the most surprising or unexpected pattern?
The most surprising pattern came from the bar chart and crosstab for “Have you ever had suicidal thoughts?” versus Depression. That visualization showed a very sharp separation between the two classes, with depression rates much higher among students who answered “Yes” than among those who answered “No.” The strength of that relationship was much larger than for most other variables and stood out immediately during EDA.

How will that insight influence your approach to feature engineering?
That insight suggests two important decisions for feature engineering. First, variables such as Academic Pressure and Financial Stress should be treated as ordered features, since the EDA showed a clear monotonic relationship with the target. Second, the suicidal thoughts variable should be handled carefully because it is highly predictive but also ethically sensitive and very close to the target concept. For that reason, it makes sense to compare models with and without this feature to see how much performance depends on it and to avoid building a model that relies too heavily on one target-proximal variable. High-cardinality variables such as City and Degree should also be grouped or encoded carefully to reduce sparsity.

Which external resource (article, video, or tool) supported your learning the most and why?
The most useful resource was the scikit-learn documentation, especially the sections on Pipeline, ColumnTransformer, SimpleImputer, and OneHotEncoder. It was the most helpful because it showed how to build a preprocessing workflow that is clean, reproducible, and safe from data leakage. That was especially important in this project, since the dataset contains both numeric and categorical features and requires consistent handling of missing values, encoding, scaling, and train-test splitting.
